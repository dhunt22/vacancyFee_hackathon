An audit of vacant parcels & untaxed market equity

Sacramento County is sitting on $3.2 billion of vacant‑parcel market value that escapes the tax roll.

Across 491,698 parcels in Sacramento County we identified 28,670 that meet a working definition of vacant — coded vacant land, zero‑improvement parcels, parking lots, and abandoned service stations. We then estimated the present‑day market value of every parcel in the county using sale prices, the FHFA Sacramento MSA House Price Index, and census‑block‑level comparable sales.

The gap between assessed value and market value — the slice Proposition 13 keeps off the tax roll — is the headline number above. It is a conservative floor.

I.The Gap

Assessed value versus market value

Proposition 13 caps annual increases in assessed value at 2 %. After decades of appreciation, the assessment roll has drifted far below what these parcels would actually sell for. The gap is largest on parcels that change hands rarely — which is to say, the ones most likely to sit vacant.

II.Where the Vacancies Are

How we counted vacant parcels

The County’s land‑use codebook flags some parcels as vacant outright, but plenty more sit underused without that label. We layer three signals to catch them.

  1. T1

    Coded Vacant

    19,364

    Sacramento land‑use codes beginning with I — explicitly vacant residential, commercial, or industrial land per the assessor’s schema.

  2. T2

    Zero Improvement

    9,151

    Parcels with $0 in improvement value — no taxable structure on the lot — excluding infrastructure, parks, agriculture, and government holdings.

  3. T3

    Parking & Abandoned

    155

    Surface parking lots (code BFH) and abandoned service stations (code BFK) — economically vacant even where a structure stands.

III.The 311 Signal

What residents are calling about

Across 1.5 million 311 calls in Sacramento County, four categories cluster strongly with each other on the same address. They form a fingerprint of neglect: board‑ups, code‑enforcement complaints, emergency housing repair, and abandoned animals.

IV.Method

How the market value was estimated

Every parcel in the county received an estimate via a four‑tier waterfall. Tiers fall back to less specific data only when better data is missing.

Tier Approach Coverage Share
A Most recent arm’s‑length sale price, scaled by FHFA Sacramento HPI 236,804 48.2 %
B Census‑block comparable $/sqft from Tier A sales (block group → tract → zip → county) 214,659 43.7 %
C County‑wide assessed‑to‑market ratio fallback for parcels missing area data 19,047 3.9 %
D Vacant‑land $/sqft × lot area for parcels with no assessed value 21,188 4.3 %

Outliers are tempered with a 5×‑assessed sanity cap; 31,556 parcels were capped (the uncapped value is preserved for audit). The original four‑tier estimator and the hybrid above were cross‑checked against an independent methodology by a teammate; agreement is high on residential and divergent on large commercial — see the compare_three_way report.
